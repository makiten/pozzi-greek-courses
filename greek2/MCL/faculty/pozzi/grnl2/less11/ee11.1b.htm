11.1b:  Use of the demonstratives

 

When you looked at the comparative chart of English and Greek demonstratives on page 11.1a, you probably wondered how to translate   οὗτος   αὕτη   τοῦτο , since it is equivalent both to this and  that.  We will apply a rule of thumb that usually works.  When there is a contrast between  ὅδε and οὗτος,  we want to represent that contrast by translating  ὅδε = "this" and οὗτος  = "that."  When, on the other hand, οὗτος  is contrasted with  ἐκεῖνος , we represent the contrast translating οὗτος = "this" and  ἐκεῖνος  = "that."  That is to say,

 

ὅδε / οὗτος                     this / that

οὗτος   /   ἐκεῖνος            this / that 

 

When they are used separately, we translate

ὅδε                      this

οὗτος                 that           

 ἐκεῖνος              that

 

Observations

When reporting someone else's words, a writer often uses ταῦτα ("those things") to refer to what precedes, and τάδε  ("these things") to anticipate what follows.  So it would be appropriate to express, as it were, "I heard what precedes (ταῦτα), and I answered what follows (τάδε)."

 

EXAMPLE

 

μή λέγε·  “τοῦτο ποιήσω ἢ ἐκεῖνο,”  ἀλλ’  ἐπεὶ  ἐποίησας, λέγε·  “ ταῦτα ἐποίησα.”

Do not say, “I will do this or that,” but, after you have done1 // it //, say, “I did that.”

Note 1:  Sometimes English style requires that we use our perfect tense instead of the simple past to translate the Greek aorist.
