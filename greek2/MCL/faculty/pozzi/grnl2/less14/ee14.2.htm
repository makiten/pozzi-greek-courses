 14.2  Second aorist active and middle

 

The first aorist is regular and therefore predictable.  You are able not only to conjugate it using the third form in a verb's citation, but if you know that a verb is regular, even if you are not given the citation, you may construct the aorist by adding to the present stem the  following tense suffix:  -σα-.  Just so a foreign learner of English may form the simple past tense "help-ed" by adding the appropriate ending to the present stem "help."  Yet...  many verbs in the English language (many, typically, very common in everyday conversation) have an irregular past tense, a "strong" past.  A "weak" past tense, such as "helped," needs the specific ending "ed" to be identified; a "strong" past, such as "went, saw, spoke," etc., does not need the help of an ending.  It is distinctive enough.

 

The same pattern applies to Greek verbs.  The "weak" aorist is most often called "first aorist."  You have studied it in the active voice:

ἔπαυσα = I stopped someone or something; and  in the middle voice: ἐπαυσάμην = I came to a halt. 

 

The "strong" aorist is called "second aorist."  Its stem is different from the present stem, and must be learned as part of the verb citation.  This is not good news, but there is a silver lining: read about its conjugation in 14.3.

Some second aorist stems differ so much from the present stems that they must be simply memorized.  An egregious example is φέρω.  Not only does it have different stem in the future: οἴσω ; it takes still another stem in the aorist, namely  ἐνεγκ-  Its second aorist is  ἤνεγκον.  Three stems, that must have been originally three independent verbs, became associated with the same meaning. 

Few verbs are so unpredictable. In many other examples it is possible at least to compare the present and second aorist stems and find a pattern of contrasts.  The second aorist may lack an element that the present stem has; it may show the leanest form of the verb al stem, while the present has an expanded form; or it may result from the present stem by "reduplicating" it (present  ἀγ- / 2nd aorist ἀγαγ- ) It is useful to group together verbs that show the same contrasting pattern:  e.g. μανθάνω, ἔμαθον  (to learn) with λαμβάνω, ἔλαβον  (to take, receive).
