18.1  Forms of the Aorist Subjunctive

All you need to learn about the Aorist Subjunctive are its forms. Since the Aorist is not a past tense outside of the Indicative Mood,1 its translation will most of the time be the same as that of the corresponding forms of the Present Subjunctive. They will differ, if at all, in their aspect.  Yet, as you know, it is not always possible to represent aspect distinctively in English. For now we will simply use the same translation for the Present and the Aorist Subjunctive (whenever a translation without a context is admissible, that is, in an independent use of the Subjunctive).  When you conjugate the Subjunctive, please do not translate the forms!

 

You learned in Lesson 17 how to recognize the Present Subjunctive (both Active and Middle-Passive), and how to form it by combining the present stem with the long thematic vowel and primary endings. To recognize and form the Aorist Subjunctive you will apply a similar formula, based on the Aorist stem of the verb. 

 

aorist stem  +  ω / η +  primary endings

 

Let us start with a verb that has a First Aorist:  γράφω, future γράψω, aorist  ἔγραψα.  We need to remember not to include the augment as we pick up the aorist stem in order to form the aorist subjunctive, since there is augment only in the Indicative.  The first aorist stem of this verb is is γραψα-, which will trade the vowel   α in the tense suffix for the long vowels of the Subjunctive.  So in practice we will be adding the long thematic vowel to  γραψ-, as shown in the chart below, where the Present Subjunctive and the Aorist Subjunctive are conjugated side by side.

This is so straightforward that I will confess I simply used the Replace function to substitute ψ for  φ and, voilà, I went from Present to Aorist Subjunctive.  The first person of  the Active Aorist Subjunctive,  γράψω, is identical to the first person of the Future Indicative.  When you read, the context will enable you to make that distinction without difficulty.   The same can be said for the other identical forms, highlighted with red.

Let us create now the Aorist Subjunctive of a verb with a Second Aorist.  This is even easier, if we follow the rule of thumb for the second aorist in forms other than the Indicative mood:  pretend you are conjugating a present, but use the second aorist stem.  The example here will be:

 

ἄγω, ἄξω, ἤγαγον.  Again, we need to remember not to include augment as we pick up the second aorist stem in order to form the aorist subjunctive.  In this case, the 2nd aorist stem is ἀγαγ-.
