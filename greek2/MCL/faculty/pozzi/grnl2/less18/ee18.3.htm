18.3  Uses of the dependent Subjunctive: Subjunctive + ἄν

 

In  17.3 you learned how to interpret a few subjunctives, used independently.  To understand now the dependent uses of the subjunctive  you will need to store away that knowledge separately and suspend those associations.  The dependent uses of the Subjunctive are  numerous, but in them the Subjunctive per se does not convey any meaning: it serves as a marker, sometimes alone, sometimes with a particle, of particular constructions.  These are mostly dependent clauses. 

In this lesson we study only one of its applications, a usage where the Subjunctive is accompanied by the particle ἄν.  In turn, we  cannot say that ἄν has any particular "meaning."  Rather, in combination with different  moods of the verb, it produces a variety of effects. 

 

Rules of thumb for the Subjunctive + ἄν

The Subjunctive with  ἄν never occurs independently.  Used in the following dependent clauses:

   temporal clauses                      conditional clauses                relative clauses

it has one of two possible effects:1

 

1) it makes a temporal, conditional, or relative clause a generalizing clause. 

Examples in English: Whenever I need her, she is (always) ready to help.  If ever I need her, she is (always) ready to help.  

Whoever needs her  (always)  finds her ready to help.

 

2) it points the respective clause to the future.

Examples in English: When I [will]2 call her, she will come.  If  I [will]2 call her, she will come.   She will welcome him who [will call] calls her.

Since the Subjunctive with ἄν has two posible effects, how can we tell which is applicable to a particular example?  Easily: by looking at the tense of the main verb.  If the main verb is in the present,  the dependent clause is generalizing; if the main verb is in the future, the dependent clause refers to the future.

Notes

1: Granted, this is a simplification: in your readings you will find examples that do not let themselves be classified so easily. 

2: Note that in English we tend to put the dependent verb, in such clauses, in the Present:  If you see him tomorrow... etc.

3: The conjunction ὅτε and  ἄν are combined in one word.  Also: ἐπειδή + ἄν = ἐπειδάν, ἐπεί + ἄν = ἐπάν, etc.

4: Note that the constructions in Ib and IIb have the same meaning (except for stylistic differences, that we will not consider for now). 

 

EXAMPLES

IIa  ὅταν σπεύδῃ τις αὐτός, καὶ ὁ θεὸς συνάπτεται.  (Aeschylus)

Whenever a man is eager himself, the god also works with him.

IIb   λέξω ... ἕως ἂν ἀκούειν βούλησθε.  (Demosthenes)

I will speak so long as you wish to listen
