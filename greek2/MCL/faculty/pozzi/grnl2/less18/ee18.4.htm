18.4  Uses of the dependent Subjunctive: Subjunctive +  ἄν, 2

 

IN CONDITIONAL CLAUSES

 

 

 

main verb in any tense

 

I  

  indicative

 (any tense)

Ia  εἴ τι καλὸν ἔχει,

Ib  εἴ τι καλὸν ἕξει,  

  μετέχει μεθ’  ἡμῶν.

  μεθέξει μεθ’  ἡμῶν.

If he has anything good, he shares it with us.

If he (will) have anything good, he will share it with us.1

II

   ἄν + the Subjunctive 3

 

IIa  ἐάν τι2 καλὸν ἔχῃ,

main verb in the present:       generalizing meaning

 

  μετέχει μεθ’  ἡμῶν.

 

If ever he has anything good, he (always) shares it with us.

IIb3  ἐάν τι2 καλὸν ἔχῃ,

main verb in the future:         future meaning

 μεθέξει μεθ’  ἡμῶν.

If he (will have) has anything good, he will share it with us.1

Notes  

1: Note that in English we tend to put the dependent verb, in such clauses, in the Present:  If you see him tomorrow... etc.

2: The conjunction εἰ and  ἄν are combined in one word: ἐάν, sometimes even ἄν with long  ᾱ  (!).

3: Note that the constructions in Ib and IIb have the same meaning (except for stylistic differences that we will not consider for now). 

 

EXAMPLES

IIa   γελᾷ ὁ μῶρος, καὶ ἐάν τι μὴ γελοῖον ᾖ.   (Menander)

A fool laughs even if something is not funny.

IIb   ἐὰν ζητῇς καλῶς, εὑρήσεις.   (Plato)

If you (will) seek well, you shall find.
