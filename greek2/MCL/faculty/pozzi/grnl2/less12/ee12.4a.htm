12.4a: Indirect or dependent statements in Accusative + Infinitive

In 12.3 we saw examples of indirect statements where the verb remained in the Indicative (if that had been the mood of the spoken expression) and often retained even the tense, person, etc.  Here we will look at examples where the verb becomes an Infinitive.  Some English verbs offer a parallel structure:  "I believe him to be honest." Just as "he" becomes "him" in English, thus in Greek the subject of an indirect statement with the infinitive is almost always in the Accusative.  No conjunction is used to introduce this kind of dependent statement.

Most Greek verbs may take either  ὅτι / ὡς  or Accusative +  Infinitive.   φημί1   may take only Accusative with Infinitive, and εἶπον, an Aorist  of  λέγω  that we will study later may take only  ὅτι / ὡς.  Other verbs meaning to say or think may take either construction.  

We start by comparing statements reported in Direct Speech with the same statements as reported indirectly with Acc + Inf.

 

direct speech

main verb                        indirect statement with acc + infinitive

 [οἱ κατήγοροι οὐκ ἔχουσι τεκμήριον]

                S                                     V                  D O

 

  The prosecutors do not have proof  = have no proof.

 

 

  [ἔφη]  

   He said (that)                                      

                          (τοὺς κατηγόρους οὐκ  ἔχειν  τεκμήριον)

                                             S in acc                                 V = infin           D O

                                          the prosecutors did not have proof  = had no proof.

  [ἀποκαλύπτεται ἡ ὀργὴ θεοῦ ]

               V                            S         gen

 

   The wrath of God is revealed.

 

 

 [νομίζουσιν]

   They think (that)

                       (ἀποκαλύπτεσθαι  τὴν ὀργὴν θεοῦ )

                                              V=  infinitive                S  in acc       gen

 

                                            the wrath of God is revealed.

 

Clearly only the subject and the verb change.  The subject is in the accusative and the verb in the infinitive.  Note that the present indicative is represented by the present infinitive in both instances.  

 

OBSERVATIONS

a) With verbs meaning "to promise," "to expect," or the like, i.e. verbs that involve a reference to the future, the future infinitive is used.

            main clause                                            indirect statement

[ ἤλπιζον οἱ Τρῶες] (τὸν Ἕκτορα  μόνον    αὐτοῖς    σωτῆρα   ἔσεσθαι).

           V                       S                             S in acc               pred acc...           I O             ...pred acc          infinitive

 

The Trojans expected Hector to be their only savior.  /  that Hector would be2 their only savior.

 

There is another observation to make about this example.  To be is a linking verb, so the structure of the sentence is A = B.  Since the subject is in the accusative, it stands to reason that the predicate will also be in the accusative.

b) When the Subject of the Indirect Statement is the same as that of the main verb and it is expressed, it is in the Nominative.  So we have here the structure

Nominative + Infinitive

            main clause                                            indirect statement

[  ἔλεγε Σωκράτης ]  (αὐτὸς οὐδὲν γιγνώσκειν).

             V                  S                        S in nom        D O             infinitive

 

Socrates used to say that he knew nothing.

 

Note 1:   φημί   belongs to a conjugation that we are not studying yet.  It is also a defective verb, i.e. it does not have all the forms.  We will use only a few forms:  φημί = I say,  φησί= he says,    ἔφην =  I said,  ἔφη = he said. 

Note 2: English expression for  the "future in the past," which is falling out of usage in American everyday speech.
