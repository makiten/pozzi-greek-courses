12.1: 1) Third declension:  "Rogue" stems ending in  -ντ

Once again we return to the general chart of the 3rd declension in 10.3, to focus on  ὁ λέων, λέοντος. 

Let us look at its complete declension:

We observe that the nominative is marked by the long vowel and that τ has dropped at the end of a word, as it necessarily happens with a dental in that position.  We obtain the general stem from the genitive, as usual: λέοντ-.  When the endings of the 3rd declension are added to this stem, nothing remarkable happens... until we come to the dative plural.  Adding -σι  to the stem λέοντ- we obtain λέοντ-σι.   Now phonetic changes will occur that we want to understand.  The combination  -ντ   drops before  σ, and as a consequence of the loss of this consonantal group, the preceding short vowel is lengthened in compensation.  You would expect ο to lengthen into its natural long, ω , but it does not happen that way.  Compensatory lengthening turns ο into ου.  That explains the dative plural.  

 

Compensatory lengthening also explains the ending of the 3rd person plural of the Present Indicative in the verbs that we are studying: the original form was  γράφ-ο-ντσι(ν):  when ντ was dropped, ο became  ου, and the result was  γράφουσι(ν).

It will be extremely useful to recognize that the combination  -ντ  always drops before  σ, and that it has many opportunities to do so in the process of declension and conjugation. The next fact to learn is that, as we have seen in  λέουσι(ν) ,  these vowels do not always change into  the long sounds that spelling would suggest (ε into  η,  ο  into  ω ).  Only short  α  changes into long α. These are all the cases of compensatory lengthening you will encounter in the third declension:
