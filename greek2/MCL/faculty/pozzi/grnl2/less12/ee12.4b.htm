12.4b: Functions and Structure of Noun Clauses   

Functions

 

Just as in the simple sentence a noun may be subject, direct object of a transitive verb, appositive, object of a preposition, etc.,  so noun clauses may perform any of those functions.  A clause that is the direct object of a verb meaning thinking or saying, is generally called indirect statement.  When the governing verb is one of commanding, the noun clause that expresses the substance of the command is called indirect command. Interrogative noun clauses are known as indirect questions.   

 

A noun clause may have any of the following functions (there are restrictions relative to the governing verb: we will study them later on):

      

       a) Object of the Main Verb 

 

[ὁ φιλόσοφος ἔλεγε] (ὅτι οὐκ ἔχομεν ἐμβαίνειν δὶς ἐς τὸν αὐτὸν ποταμόν.)1

                   S                   V                                            V           compl infin      adv   ........ ἐς / εἰς  +   acc      ..........    

 

The philosopher used to say / said that we are not able to step into the same river twice.

 

[ ὁ φιλόσοφος ἔφη] (τὸν δαίμονα σημαίνειν ἡμῖν).2

                   S           V                        S in acc                  V =  infin            I O  

 

The philosopher said (that) the divinity gives / gave signals (“signifies / signified”) to us.  

      

       b)  Subject of the Main Verb (usually impersonal)

 

[δεῖ] (ἄξιον ἄνδρα   ἔργοις    τὴν γνώμην φαίνειν).

         V .......   S in acc    ........     dat of means               D O                   V= infin

 

It is necessary (that) a worthy man show his thought with actions. / It is necessary for a worthy man to show…

 

       c)  Appositive clarifying a noun or pronoun

 

[διὰ τί   ἀεὶ λέγεις τόδε,] (ὅτι ὁ Σωκράτης ἀδικεῖ);

 διά  + acc    adv         V          D O        conj                   S                        V             

 

Why do you always say this, (namely) that Socrates acts unjustly / does wrong?

 

Structure

 

INDIRECT STATEMENTS MAY HAVE ONE OF THE FOLLOWING STRUCTURES: 

  

           a)   ὅτι, ὡς      +     CONJUGATED VERB                                      12.3

           

b) SUBJECT IN THE ACCUSATIVE       +         INFINITIVE VERB            12.4a

 

c) SUBJECT IN THE ACCUSATIVE       +         PARTICIPLE:   to be studied later

 

Note 1:  ἔχω + Infinitive  = to be able to...   (Adapted from the famous saying by Heracleitus. )  

Note 2: Adapted from the well-known claim by Socrates that his conscience warned him when he was about to act wrongly. “It” is an inevitable addition in English.  In Greek the noun clause is the subject of the impersonal verb  δεῖ.
