12.5a: The Horror of Hiatus.  Contracted verbs

Hiatus is the gap between two vowels: the Greeks disliked it.  That is why, as you have seen, they cut ("elided") a final vowel before a word beginning with a vowel (oh, those apostrophes all over the place...):   δὲ ἐγώ   becomes δ’ ἐγώ,  ἐλπίδα ἔχει becomes ἐλπίδ’ ἔχει, etc.).  If a verbal stem ends in a vowel and is followed (as is the case in the present and imperfect) by the thematic or linking vowel, the hiatus is avoided: the vowels are contracted. Learning the results of these contractions makes the study of Greek grammar much easier. 

We start with one group of verbs, those that have a stem ending in  ε . An example is   πονέ-ω  = to toil. First I list the results of contracting ε with the sounds it encounters in the endings of present and imperfect, both active and middle-passive.  The rules can be efficiently simplified as follows:

ε  + ε > ει

ε +  ο  >  ου

ε +  long sound  (long vowel or diphthong:  η, ω, ου, ει > that long sound (long vowel or diphthong:   η, ω, ου, ει)

Let us conjugate  πονέ-ω = toil in the active voice, and  αἱρέ-ο-μαι  = choose, in the middle.  To learn these contractions make them yourself before looking at the resulting form.
