12.5b: Accents in contractions. Contracted futures

A good point of departure would be to review the very basic principles of accentuation that we learned in 7.3 . Here are a few specific rules for accents in contractions.  They will help you recognize and produce contracted forms.  For complete conjugation in the active and middle-passive present, click here for the chart.   

If one of the two vowels contracted has an acute accent, the contraction will have circumflex, if the general laws of limitation allow it: 

  

EXAMPLES 

φιλέ-ω > φιλῶ  = to love

Present indicative, 3rd sg:    φιλέ-ει > φιλεῖ   but

Present imperative 2nd sg:   φίλε-ε > φίλει     

Present imperative 3rd sg:    φιλε-έ-τω > φιλείτω   because a circumflex cannot fall on the penult if the ultima is long

Contracted futures

If you take a look at any of the recent vocabularies, you will notice that the future of some verbs is cited as ending in   -έω > ῶ, i.e. their future is like the present of contracted verbs that we are studying in this lesson.  Can we predict that a verb will exhibit this unsettling trait?  Yes, and it is not difficult.  Most verbs that take a contract future have either:

 

a) a present stem ending in what traditionally are called "liquid" consonants:  μ ν λ ῥ, or

  • ἀγγέλλω, ἀγγελέω > ἀγγελῶ  = to announce.  (The discrepancy between  λλ  in the present and  λ  in the future has a phonetic explanation which is not pertinent here.)

 

b) a present ending in  -ίζω

  • νομίζω, νομιέω > νομιῶ  = to consider, deem

 

We have come across one example of a deponent verb with a present stem ending in  ν , and it has a contracted future:

ἀποκρίνομαι, ἀποκρινέομαι > ἀποκρινοῦμαι   = to answer
