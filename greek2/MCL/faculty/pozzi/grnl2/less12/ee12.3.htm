12.3: 1)  Indirect statements with ὅτι, ὡς

2) Introduction to indirect questions

 

Indirect speech: statements  

Indirect or reported statements are introduced in Greek by many, but not all, verbs meaning thinking or knowing, saying, perceiving,  followed  by the conjunctions   ὅτι (= that), or  ὡς   The latter usually conveys the suggestion that the person who reports what was said does not necessarily subscribe to the opinion expressed or vouch for the veracity of the report. 

In written English several adjustments are made when a statement is reported indirectly, e.g. "He told me on Saturday that he would call me yesterday, but he did not."  Pronouns, tenses, adverbs if appropriate, a number of specifications are reported as relative to the persons, the time, and the place of the reporting.  In American popular English, particularly, such adjustments are heard less and less.  Instead of the report above in this paragraph, we hear, e.g. "He told me on Saturday that he will call me..."

This vivid style of reporting is one found  both in ancient and koinê Greek, even in the best prose.   The grammar and the style of Indirect statements are very interesting, but for the time being I will limit myself to one (the simplest) construction.  

 

EXAMPLE

                            main clause                                                     dependent clause:  indirect statement

[ οἱ κήρυκες ἔλεξαν τοῖς φύλαξιν ] (ὅτι βούλονται   διαλέγεσθαι    τῷ στρατηγῷ)

               S                          V                       I O                              conj              V                     complem  infinitive                   I O                                           

The heralds said to the guards that they wanted to talk with the general.

 

Note: what the heralds said was "we want," in the present.  In the English indirect statement  the present has been changed to a past tense.  This change is one that would never happen in Greek.  

 

Indirect speech: questions

 

Indirect questions are introduced not only by  verbs meaning asking or answering but also wondering, knowing, and the like. The following question in English: "They asked me, 'What do you want?' " may be reported as "They asked me what I wanted."  Some adjustments have been made and in writing a question mark is not used.  The syntax of indirect questions in Greek is rather simple, and here I limit myself to the basic form, where the indirect question may be introduced by the same interrogative used in the original question, and few or even no adjustments are made in pronouns, tenses, etc.

 

EXAMPLE

The following direct questions:

[ τί  ἔλεξεν ὁ Σωκράτης πρὸ τοῦ θανάτου;]  [τίνες ἦσαν οἱ λόγοι αὐτοῦ;] 

What did Socrates say before his death?  What were his words?  

 

may be reported without any adjustment, even if the main verb is a past tense, e.g.

 

     main clause                dependent clause:  indirect question

[ ἠρώτησε  ] (τί ἔλεξεν ὁ Σωκράτης πρὸ τοῦ θανάτου)

He asked what Socrates had said before his death.

 

                             main clause                                dependent clause:  indirect question

[ ἐβούλοντο μανθάνειν]  (τίνες  ἦσαν οἱ λόγοι αὐτοῦ.)

They wanted to learn what his words were / had been.
