29.1:  Athematic verbs: Aorist Imperative, Infinitive, Participles 

 

The most efficient way to learn the Aorist of athematic verbs if by considering the corresponding present forms and then focussing  on the contrasts.   So I suggest that you open the Chart of lesson 28, or print it out, and compare with the chart below.   In the active voice you do not need to learn the aorist forms of ἵστημι, because it has a root aorist, studied in  22.1. 

 

The Aorist Active of τίθημι, ἵημι, and δίδωμι was studied in three moods in  26.1.  Setting aside the irregular three persons of the Indicative in these aorists, we learned to spot aorist forms because they lack the -ι  reduplication of the present.  In the Imperative, Infinitive, and Participle of the Aorist, there will also be a few forms that will transgress the norm... I will highlight them in purple.

Active Aorist Participles will result from omitting reduplication of the present

                        ACTIVE AORIST PARTICIPLE

The explanation given for the active present participle applies to the active aorist participle too:  active present participles of μι-verbs with stem   ε / η  are similar to  the number one  in the masculine and neuter (εἷς, ἕν)though not with the same accent.   That is the result of :  stem +  mark of participle  + ς of the nominative.  As you know, the group ντ- drops (ALWAYS!)  before ς, and there is compensatory lengthening into ει  , not the natural long η.  The masculine and neuter of δίδωμι show the result of the same process with the stem vowel  ο/ω: for compensation ο lengthens into -ου, not the natural long  ω. As to the active aorist participle of ἵστημι , it is again the form expected in a root aorist formation.

 

The easiest way to form the feminine is by adding α to the masculine nominative and of course declining it as 1st declension with impure α .  τιθεῖσα, -ης; ἱεῖσα. ης;  διδοῦσα, ης;   ἱστᾶσα, ης;
