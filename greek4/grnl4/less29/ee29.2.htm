29.2: Accents in verbs

 

Accent is recessive in verbs: it moves as far back as the rules of limitation will allow.  

 

Examples:  πιστεύουσι<ν>, ἐπίστευον, ἀπεδίδουν, ἀπόδος, εἰσῆλθον, συνῆσαν, πάρεστι

 

Alas, there are numerous exceptions to general rules.

 

Some infinitives carry the accent on the penult: 

  • all infinitives ending in –ναι, such as πεπαιδευκέναι, παιδευθῆναι, ἱστάναι
  • the infinitive of the active first aorist: παιδεῦσαι, ἀναμεῖναι
  • the infinitive of the middle second aorist: λιπέσθαι, γενέσθαι
  • the middle/passive perfect infinitive: γεγενῆσθαι

 

Participles, since they are adjectives,  behave like nominal forms, that is, have persistent accent:

παιδεύων, παιδεύουσα, παιδεῦον 

 

But note the following quirks:

Some participles are oxytone (acute on the ultima) in the masculine and neuter:

·        active second aorist participles: ἰδών, ἰδοῦσα, ἰδόν

·        participles of the 3rd declension that end in in the masculine, with the exception of the active first aorist (παιδεύσας): παιδευθείς, παιδευθεῖσα,παιδευθέν

and

      The middle/passive perfect participle is always paroxytone (acute on penult): γεγενημένος, η, ον

Special anomalous accents in -μι  verbs

·        φημί and εἰμί are enclitic, with the exception of φής and εἶ

·        Limits to the recessive accent:

o       athematic optatives do not allow the accent to recede past the diphthong that contains the sign of the optative, i.e. iota:  τιθεῖο, διδοῖτο

o       the optative of the aorist pasive shows the same limitation:  πιστευθεῖμεν

o       ἀπέσται,  ἐπέσται do not have recessive accent

 

·        But  δύναμαι, ἐπίσταμαι, ἐπριάμην, and a few others, have recessive accent in subjunctive and optative:  δύνωμαι, πρίαιτο

Special accent rules for compound verbs

In compound verbs the recessive accent does not go past

·        augment or reduplication: παρῆγον

·        the last syllable of a preverb:  μετάδος

·        the last preverb if there is more than one  συνέκδος join in giving out   προσεπίθες
