32.2: Adverbs and Particles[1]

 

Why associate them in a single title? Because they have a comparable origin: adverbs, linguists tell us, are stereotyped forms of particular cases of nouns and pronouns.  As in English, adverbs modify a verb (to feel well), an adjective (most  proud) or another adverb (very suddenly).  Particles, on the other hand, were originally adverbs and in turn they became fixed, usually in the function of affecting an entire sentence or giving emphasis to a particular word. 

 

Adverbs: recognition

1) Numerous adverbs of manner derive from adjectives.  In English we commonly mark such adjectives with the suffix –ly: promptly, ideally.  These Greek adverbs are easily recognized because they end in -ως, with the same accent as the genitive plural of the adjective.  But knowing an adjective we cannot assume that such an adverb also existed.

2) The neuter accusative singular and / or the neuter accusative plural are used as adverbs (again, we cannot assume that this applies to every adjective!)

 

Examples:  μόνον (only),  πολύ (much, a lot),  πρῶτον (first, in the first place),  πολλά  (many times, very much)

 

3) We recognize some adverbs of manner that have the form of the dative sg of a noun:  δημοσίᾳ  (publicly),  κοινῇ  (in common, jointly)

 

4) Adverbs of place may have a suffix that indicates direction toward: -δε /-ζε /-σε , direction from: -θεν, place at: -θι, -σι, -ι.

 

Examples:   οἴκαδε (homeward), οἴκοθεν (from the home), ἐντεῦθεν (from there, thence), ἄλλοθι  (elsewhere)

Again, these are old declensional endings that were preserved with some names of cities or regions:  Ἀθήναζε, Ἀθήνηθεν

 

Comparative and Superlative Degrees of Adverbs

The comparative of an adverb is the same as the neuter accusative singular of the comparative of the adjective:

 

εὐδαιμονέστερον =  more happily         

 

The superlative is the same as the neuter accusative plural of the superlative of the adjective:

 

εὐδαιμονέστατα  =  most happily    

 

When adverbs do not derive from an adjective, their degrees of comparison  may be marked with the comparative and superlative of μάλα (= much):   μᾶλλον  (= more) and μάλιστα  (= most)

Particles

 

Prepositions and conjunctions have as much right to be called “particles” as the smaller group of mini-words listed below.  In the past, they were often all lumped together as “particles.”  But we will follow the current consensus.  To repeat the description on top of this page: particles were originally adverbs but they became fixed in the function of affecting an entire sentence or giving emphasis to a particular word. 

 

Interpreting the effects of particles and their combinations and rendering them in translation is an art that can be mastered only after extensive reading.  A good strategy to get a “feel” of the particle or particles in a text is to start disregarding those that do not have an obvious effect, and to reconsider them once the rest of the text has been understood.  If at that point you are still at a loss, look up the definitions and examples in Liddell-Scott-Jones.[2]  And remember that often it is best to omit in translation the subtle effect of the particles that may dot a text. 

 

A short list of examples:

 

ἄν  limits the meaning of the moods. It has two distinct uses, with the Subjunctive (see ) and with the Optative (see 18.3, 18.4, 18.5, 22.2).

 

ἄρα marks a thought as following from another one (compare English non-temporal “then”, used as a weaker form of “therefore.”) See below οὐ̂ν, which is stronger and marks a consequence drawn from facts.

ἆρα (notice the circumflex on the long -α) is a neutral way of introducing a question, without assuming either a positive or a negative answer.   It should not be translated.

αὐ̂ (postpositive), an adversative particle meaning “on the other hand,” “on the contrary.”  Compare Latin autem.

γάρ  (postpositive)  usually stands in the second position of the clause it marks.  The English formal explanatory “for” is a good equivalent, even though it is now rare in conversation in English.

 

γέ (postpositive and enclitic) often denotes the kind of emphasis that would be best rendered by intonation.  In a written translation, “certainly” might be excessive.   Sometimes the effect of γέ is restrictive, as in “at least,” “at any rate.”

 

δέ (postpositive)  marks a weak contrast or a transition. 

 

The pair μέν... δέ points more visibly than δέ alone to a contrast, a parallel, or a sequence.  The translation “on the one hand... on the other hand,” while often accurate, burdens the English prose.  Translate the pair as “and” or “but,” or introduce the μέν segment with “While...” and make the δέ segment a main clause.

 

δή (postpositive)  = indeed, certainly, in fact.  It may emphasize a single word or an expression.  (Smyth says: It sets forth what is obvious, acknowledged, and natural, and often corresponds to voilà.)  Examples:  μόνος δή quite alone, ὀλίγοι δή very few; οὕτω δή,  just so.

 

εἰ γὰρ as an interjection marks the expression of a wish = would that..., if only...

μήν (postpositive) emphasizes either a whole statement or a single word. It may be asseverative, in truth, surely or adversative, especially after a negative, yet, however.

μέν, originally a weaker form of μήν, was sometimes used by itself (μέν solitarium), with the antithetical clause with δέ  left implicit.

μέντοι (postpositive) = nevertheless

 

οὖν  (postpositive)  is inferential =  then, well then, therefore, accordingly. It marks a transition to a new thought and continues a narrative (often after ἐπεί, ἐπειδή, ὅτε), resumes an interrupted narration, and in general states a conclusion or inference

 

οὔκουν and οὐκου̂ν should be distinguished by their accent: οὔκουν, usually at the beginning of its clause, emphasizes the negative, so it may be translated “therefore not, so not, at any rate . . . not, surely not,” but in οὐκου̂ν the negative meaning fades away and it becomes equivalent to the simple οὖν .

 

τὲ... καί unites similar and opposite elements.  It is weaker than καὶ ... καί.

 

τοί  (postpositive and enclitic) was once the dative of the second person singular pronoun.  Its direct meaning would thus be equivalent to “Let me tell you.” Translate: in truth, surely, you (must) know.
