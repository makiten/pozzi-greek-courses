32.3  Enclitics

 

Have you noticed the following apparent violations of accent rules in Greek texts you read?  Sometimes the acute accent on the ultima does not become grave if another word follows it but remains acute: δός μοι.  And what about words with two accents, such as παῖδές τινες? 

 

The general rules are broken because these words are followed by an enclitic (from ἐνκλίνω , lean on). Enclitics do not have an accent of their own but lean on the preceding word.

 

Examples of enclitics are: indefinite pronouns and adverbs, the present of εἰμί and φημί except for the 2nd person sg, and many particles.  Some personal pronouns have both accented and enclitic forms.

NOTE:  When several enclitics occur in succession, each receives an accent from the following, only the last having no accent:

τί should not be confused with the interrogative: it is the neuter of the indefinite.  It supports another enclitic, and for that reason it carries an accent.
