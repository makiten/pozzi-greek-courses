35.1: The style of koinê

When referring to the style of the common language (κοινὴ διάλεκτος) of Hellenistic times, it is important to recognize that, because of the commonality of this language and its geographical as well as historical scope, we are referring to many variants, both written and spoken.  

 

As stated early in the course (see Introduction 0.1.1), since its origin Greek developed in diverse regional forms or dialects. Of these, a form of Ionic known as Attic prevailed over others because of the political, economic, and cultural hegemony of Athens  (in Attica). In what we call the classical period (5th and 4th centuries BCE), Attic merged gradually with the local dialects used in other parts of Greece.  In other words, an early common form of Greek was being shaped.  Attic was its principal ingredient because Athens, while still a separate city-state, had become, by the fifth century BCE, the head of a virtual empire.  Consider the current globalization of English.

 

Philip of Macedonia, in the fourth century, adopted Greek as the official language of his own state.  His son, Alexander  the Great, expanded by military conquest the power of the Macedonian Empire throughout much of the known world.  Having been a pupil of Aristotle, power-thirsty Alexander was proud to act as the carrier of a superior civilization.  Among the numerous cities he founded and named after himself, the Egyptian Alexandria actually became, in the third century, thanks to its renowned Library, the most important center of  Hellenistic culture.  

 

Koinê Greek is not just the language used to translate the Old Testament and to set the New Testament in writing.  Nor was it spoken only by the uneducated populus.  Some scholars distinguish a transitional period when middle and upper classes spoke their local dialects at home, and for a limited time continued to write them.  Eventually, in the new Hellenistic kingdoms, it became the spoken language and the single written standard of the Greaco-Macedonian aristocracy.  Naturally, it was learned by non-native speakers who mingled with it a variety of elements of their own dialects. 

 

Just so nowadays English influences local languages with (some inevitable) Anglicisms, but local idioms, vocabularies, and usages, are integrated into English so that it sounds less and less like English.  Pronunciation is a hybrid, different around the world depending on the speakers nationality, education, etc., etc.  Koinê Greek was, therefore, an umbrella for a rich variety of languages that it is a challenge to describe.

 

In lesson 34 I listed some of the contrasts between classical and postclassical Greek.  The form of koinê that is the focus in these courses is that of the Septuagint and the New Testament.  For more information on the text of the Bible, you may consult the initial chapters of The Story of the Bible, by Frederic G. Kenyon (1936), accessible as one of the resources of Michael Marlowes  Bible Research.  It is important to read the Preface  to the electronic edition of Kenyons book and understand that it preceded the discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls and, of course, many recent scholarly contributions and discussions.
