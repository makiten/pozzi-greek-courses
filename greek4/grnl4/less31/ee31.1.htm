31.1   οἶδα

  FORMS OF οἶδα

 

οἶδα means “I know,” yet it is not a present.  It is a second perfect based on the same  stem as εἶδον, i.e. ἰδ- (originally ϝιδ-, cf Latin video).  It seems to point to the origin of knowledge in sensorial perception, since the stem means to see; so οἶδα  I have seen, therefore I know. 

 

Looking at its forms as a perfect helps us understand them.  But if we call it a “second perfect” we do not do it justice...  Not only does it lack κ , as do, for instance, λέληθα, πέπομφα  etc.  οἶδα is an archaic form of the perfect, without reduplication.  Well, that, and the phonetic transformation of δ + another dental into σ explain the Indicative.  But how to explain the different initial vowels in the other moods?  Let us take them one by one.

 

In the Imperative, the basic stem  ϝιδ- loses the digamma and, expectedly, changes a dental into sigma.  In the Subjunctive and Optative, as well as in the nominal forms of Infinitive and Participle, we note the alternance of vowels that is a common feature of Greek (as in λείπω, λέλοιπα) If we take the stem εἰδ-   and add to it the endings of a thematic verb, we obtain easily the remaining forms.

 

In the Imperfect (Pluperfect as to form) we see augment and reduplication:  εἰδ-  becomes  ᾔδ- .

SYNTAX OF οἶδα: verbs of “perception”

 

The list of verbs we could classify as “verbs of perception” in earlier lessons was limited  (15.5, p1 and 17.4 B1).  We should expand it now: it includes not only verbs denoting perception by the senses but also those that represent mental understanding, learning, knowing, even showing, that is, actions inherently associated with perception.  Noun clauses that make explicit the object or the substance of that perception or understanding have two possible constructions:

 

a)  ὅτι, ὡς  + a regular indirect statement or question

 

ουδεὶς οὐκ ᾔδει ὅτι Διογένης πλούτου οὐκ ἐπιθυμεῖται.    Everybody knew that Diogenes did not desire wealth.

 

b)  a participial clause “of perception,” with the subject in the acc (unless the verb governs a special case) + a verbal predicate consisting in a participle agreeing with the subject

 

εὖ ἴστε τοὺς συμμάχους νικηθέντας  You (pl) know well that your allies have been defeated (or “Know well (you pl) that...”)

 

When the participle is a predicate of the Subject of the Verb, it is in the nominative:

 

εὖ ἴστε νικηθέντες  You (pl) know well that you have been defeated  (or “Know well (you pl) that...”)
