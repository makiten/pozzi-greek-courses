31.2b  Verbal Adjectives

 

Only transitive verbs may have verbal adjectives, and not all of them do in attested texts.  Those that exist are based on the present verbal stem (e.g. γράφ-) and two suffixes, -τός, -τή, -τόν and  -τέος, -τέα, -τέον.  Both result in Type I adjectives, with a specific meaning for each suffix.   

a) γράφ- + -τός, -τή, -τόν > γραπτός, ή, όν  = able to be drawn or written (the action denoted by the verb is possible)

b) + -τέος, -τέα, -τέον  > γραπτέος, γραπτέα, γραπτέον  = that must be drawn or written (the action denoted by the verb is necessary or obligatory)

Clearly both adjectives envisage the verbal action from a passive point of view (the second can be compared to the Latin gerundive); that is why only transitive verbs may serve for the formation of verbal adjectives. 

Syntax of the verbal adjectives.

a) A verbal adjective ending in τός, -τή, -τόν  has the syntax of an adjective

b) For -τέος, -τέα, -τέον adjectives, two constructions are possible, personal and impersonal.  In both the verb “to be” is expressed or implicit.

·        in the personal construction, the adjective agrees with a passive subject:

ὠφηλετέα σοι ἡ πόλις ἐστί  the State must be benefited by you; οἱ συμμαχεῖν ἐθέλοντες εὖ ποιητέοι  those who would be allies must be well treated

·        in the impersonal construction, the necessity of the action is emphasized.  The verbal adjective is in the neuter nominative, singular or, less frequently, plural.  Any verbal complement may follow, but if the agent is expressed, it must be in the dative.  Examples:

       πειστέον πατρὸς λόγοις I must obey my father's commands;  τῷ ἀδικοῦντι δοτέον δίκην the wrong-doer must suffer punishment
