28.2: Accents in declension

 

It is hardly necessary to remind you that the study of accents has been delayed in this course.  Mea culpa.  Perhaps when you read further you will agree that it was prudent...

 

A note concerning the genitive and dative of articles and nouns of the 1st and 2nd declensions was introduced in 5.3, and the rules of limitation of the accent are in 7.5.  We started by learning that nominal forms (articles, nouns, adjectives, participles), that is, declinable parts of speech, wish to keep the accent always wherever it was in the nominative singular and wish to retain its nature (acute or circumflex).  That is to say, in nouns and other nominal parts of speech, accents are persistent.  The particular position of the accent in a nominative singular is determined by etymology, by general tendencies in the language, or by imponderables.  We must accept the accent of the nominative singular as a fact, as one of the features of the vocabulary item in question. 

 

When the endings change, and often with them, the number of syllables of a declined word changes,  the accent must respect the rules of limitation of 7.5.  These rules are valid throughout the language, not just for  particular parts of speech:

 

·        the acute cannot be farther from the end of the word than the antepenult, and only if the ultima is short;

·        the circumflex (which can only fall on a vowel long by nature or a diphthong) cannot be farther from the end of the word than the penult, and only if the ultima is short.

 

In addition to the rules of limitation, there is a rule that prescribes an obligatory circumflex on a long penult if  the ultima is short:  This is true throughout the language, not just for  particular parts of speech

 

 

HOW DO THE RULES OF LIMITATION WORK?  In declension, where accents are persistent,

 

·        a circumflex will change its nature (to acute): nominative μοῦσα, genitive μούσης, nominative δῶρον, genitive δώρου.  

 

·        an acute will move closer to the end: nominative θάλασσα, genitive θαλάσσης, nominative ἀπόστολος, genitive ἀποστόλου.

 

If that were all, accents would be a breeze.  But there are specific rules in the particular declensions, which take precedence over the general rules, including the notion that accents are persistent in nominal forms.  Well, sometimes they are not.  We need to consider the article and then the declensions separately. 

 

ARTICLE (See the chart)

 

In the three genders the article changes from acute to circumflex in genitive and dative, both singular and plural.

 

NOUNS

 

FIRST AND SECOND DECLENSION “RULES” AGAINST THE RULES 

Charts: 1st declension, 2nd declension

 

The nominative/voc plural endings -αι and -οι are considered short for the purposes of accent (not so for meter), so the following nominative/voc forms are legitimate:  θάλασσαι, ἀπόστολοι

 

As does the article, paroxytones (acute on the last syllable) change their acute into a circumflex in the genitive and dative of singular and plural.

 

Examples of nouns: nominative  ἰατρός, genitive  singular  ἰατροῦ / genitive  plural ἰατρῶν; nominative  σοφός, genitive  singular σοφοῦ / genitive  plural σοφῶν

 

FIRST DECLENSION SPECIFIC ACCENTS

 

In feminine as well as in masculine nouns of the 1st declension, the genitive plural is always  -ῶν.

It is the result of a contraction (α+ων, ε+ων, therefore it follows the rules of accents in contractions (See page) 

 

Nouns ending in -της    have a vocative  with ᾰ  (short alpha) ending: nominative  πολίτης, voc πολῖτα   and, with  recessive accent (!) ,  nominative δεσπότης, voc δέσποτα 

 

“TYPE ONE” [1]  ADJECTIVES

 

Generally the accents are like those of the nouns in each gender... BUT “TYPE ONE” FEMININE ADJECTIVES   do not behave as indicated above for nouns,  but take the same accent as the masculine genitive plural:

 

nominative      ἡμέτερος,   ἡμετέρα,    ἡμέτερον

genitive plural   ἡμετέρων,  ἡμετέρων,  ἡμετέρων

 

 

THIRD DECLENSION NOUNS

 

·        Monosyllabic nouns throw to the winds the “persistent” accent and move it to the ending in genitive and dative, both singular and plural:  κλώψ, κλωπός

 

·        Proper names in -ης have recessive accent in the vocative: nominative Σωκράτης, vocative  Σώκρατες

Type 2a  if the adjective has three syllables like  εὐδαίμων , in the neuter nominative/accusative the accent recedes one syllable: εὔδαιμον

 

PRONOUNS

 

The interrogative  always bears an acute accent on ί: τίς τίνος τίσι(ν)  .  This accent does not turn to grave if another word follows, as the general rule prescribes.

 

The indefinite is enclitic, and it never receives an accent on ι .  The rules of accent in enclitics will be presented in a later lesson.
