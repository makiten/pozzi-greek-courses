28.3:  Prepositional phrases with the accusative[1]

Surely you remember the spatial values of the three cases presented in lesson 6, with a chart that will serve as introduction to this expanded study of prepositional phrases in lessons 28, 29, and 30. As a matter of fact, since prepositions, scholars tell us, were originally adverbs of place, many of their metaphorical uses can be derived logically from the basic locative or directional (toward and from) values of the three cases that accompany prepositions. 

So, when you do not perceive immediately the meaning of  a prepositional phrase in a reading, you will find it useful to consider its basic spatial connotation, or a likely parallel application to time.  It should be pointed out that this doesn’t always provide an answer.  Languages are too creative and unpredictable to be boxed into rigid categories. 

Although in this lesson our concern is with prepositional phrases with the accusative, we need to start by listing, 1)  those which can only take the accusative, 2) those that may take accusative or genitive, and 3) prepositions that may accompany three cases: accusative, dative, or genitive.

1)  ἀνά, εἰς, ὡς as a preposition,   occur with the accusative only.  Here “direction toward” is clear:

ἀνά +  ACCUSATIVE

·        Space: to a higher point: ἀνὰ τὸν ποταμόν  upstream (cp. κατὰ τὸν ποταμόν downstream); extension in place:  ἀνὰ πᾶσαν τὴν γῆν: over the whole earth

·        Extension in Time: ἀνὰ νύκτα  through the night

·        Distributive:  ἀνὰ ἑκατὸν ἄνδρας  by hundreds, ἀνὰ πᾶσαν ἡμέραν daily . Manner:  ἀνὰ λόγον proportionately

εἰς  +  ACCUSATIVE

·         Space: εἰς τὴν πόλιν ἰέναι  to go into / to the city: b. place and time: εἰς ἐνιαυτόν  up to, in a year

·         With verbs of saying or speaking, εἰς relates to the persons to or before whom one speaks, εἰς τὸν δῆμον λέγειν; purpose:  χρήματα ἀναλίσκειν εἰς τὴν στρατείαν  to spend money on the military

·         With an acc such as “the house,” “the abode,” “the realm” implicit, it takes a genitive:  ἐς τοῦ διδασκάλου to the teacher’s (house or school)

ὡς  +  ACCUSATIVE, used only with reference to persons, ἥκω ὡς ὑμᾶς  I have come to / before you (pl)

2) The following prepositions may take the accusative and genitive:  ἀμφί, διά, κατά, μετά, ὑπέρ

ἀμφί  +  ACCUSATIVE

·        Place, about, around, (compare with περί), on both sides, mostly with a sense of motion; of persons grouped about one:   νόμους ζῆν to live according / by the laws

μετά   +  ACCUSATIVE

·        Time: μετὰ ταῦτα  thereupon, thereafter

ὑπέρ   +  ACCUSATIVE

·       Place, in reference to motion: over, beyond,  ὑπὲρ ἄνθρωπον φρονεῖν to have thoughts (= ambitions) “beyond a mortal” = excessive for a mortal

3) Prepositions used with three cases, accusative, genitive, and dative: ἐπί, παρά, περί, πρός, ὑπό

ἐπί   +  ACCUSATIVE

·        Place, upon or on to a height, with verbs of motion: καταφεύγειν ἐπὶ τὰ ὄρη to flee to the mountains;  in hostile sense, against, πέμπειν στρατηγὸν ἐπί τινας ; of the object or purpose for which one goes,  ἐπὶ  βοῦν ἴτω let him go for an ox

παρά   +  ACCUSATIVE

·        Place, beside, near, by,  with verbs of coming, going, etc., to the side of, to  φοιτᾶν παρὰ τὸν Σωκράτη to visit Socrates frequently;  

·        generally, of comparison, alongside of, compared with, usually implying superiority, παρά  τὰ ἄλλα ζῷα ὥσπερ θεοὶ ἄνθρωποι βιοτεύουσι; distributively, e.g. of Time, τὸ παρ' ἑκάστην ἡμέραν ἡδύ

  +  ACCUSATIVE

·        Place, properly of the object round about which motion takes place, yet also without motion: δεῖ φυλακὰς παρὰ τὸ στρατόπεδον εἰ̂ναι we ought to have guards around our camp;

·        about, in the case of, (compar ἀμφί )

πρός  +  ACCUSATIVE

  • Place, towards, to, with verbs of motion, ἀπῆλθε πρὸς ἑαυτόν returned to his home; ιn hostile sense, against, ἐπιέναι πρὸς τοὺς πολεμίους  to attack the enemy;
  • of Time, towards or near a certain time, at or about, πρός τὸ παρόν for the moment
  • in reference to, in respect of, touching, τὰ πρὸς  τὸν πόλεμον military matters

ὑπό   +  ACCUSATIVE

of Place; to express motion towards and under an object,  ὑπὸ τὰ τείχη φεύγειν to flee to the foot of the Great Wall

 


[1]  A comprehensive review of prepositional phrases is not intended.  Once again, I am choosing the most common and basic structures, which will be pliable to interpretation in the innumerable contexts where they appear.  The special features of the use of prepositional phrases in Biblical Greek will be presented later.
