`30.2  Passive deponents and other anomalies in the meanings of voices

In lesson 8.4b you made the acquaintance of deponent verbs in the present system.  We may return now to the first example given, γί<γ>νομαι, and consider its complete citation:

γί<γ>νομαι, γενήσομαι, ἐγενόμην, γεγένημαι / γέγονα

Aside from γέγονα (an alternative active perfect) this verb is consistently deponent.  Its four systems (present, future, second aorist, perfect) are conjugated in the middle voice but convey an active meaning: to become, come to be or be born.

Other verbs that we needed to use already in the early lessons had in store for us several surprises.  We list these anomalies here and hint at some common traits that may help anticipate the irregular uses of the voices.[1]

ACTIVE PRESENTS

·        some active presents have a deponent future.  Here we find verbs denoting a physical activity, especially the activities of the senses:  ἀκούω, ἀκούσομαι (later ἀκούσω) to hear; γιγνώσκω, γνώσομαι to recognize, know;  μανθάνω, μαθήσομαι  = to learn; τρέχω, δραμέομαι > δραμοῦμαι = to run.

·        there are active presents that take a middle future with passive meaning: ἀδικέω to wrong, ἀδικήσομαι I will be wronged; ἐάω to permit, ἐάσομαι I will be allowed ; ὀνειδίζω to reproach, ὀνειδιοῦμαι I will be rebuked; τρέφω to nourish, θρέψομαι I will be nourished; φιλέω to love, φιλήσομαι I will be loved;  φυλάττω to guard, φυλάξομαι I will be guarded. 

o       The worst culprits in this group have two forms for the passive future meaning:  τῑμάω to honor, τῑμήσομαι, τῑμηθήσομαι, both meaning I will be honored.

MIDDLE DEPONENT PRESENTS: How do verbs with middle deponent presents behave in the aorist?

 

·        MIDDLE DEPONENT AORISTS

some deponents show future or aorist stems consistent with their present, i.e. with an active meaning represented by middle forms.  These verbs are called  middle deponents: ἀποκρίνομαι, ἀπεκρινάμην to reproach;  δέχομαι, ἐδεξάμην to receive;  ἐργάζομαι, εἰργασάμην to work, labor; ἡγέομαι, ἡγήσομαι to lead, consider; θεάομαι, θεάσομαι to behold.

 

·        PASSIVE DEPONENT AORISTS

other deponent verbs do not take, in the aorist, forms of the middle passive but those of the aorist passive.  The meaning is active.  These are known as passive deponents.  Verbs in this group usually refer to mental or psychological activity: βούλομαι, ἐβουλήθην to wish, οἴομαι, ᾠήθην to think. 

 

... AND THE UNPREDICTABLES

 

There is a list of rogues here too: some verbs sport both a middle (deponent) future and a regular active future:

ἀκούω, ακούσομαι / ἀκούσω

Finally, a pattern that defies classification; luckily only one example is found in prose with any frequency: ἁλίσκομαι, to be captured, provides a passive voice to αἱρέω, to capture.  The present ἁλίσκομαι has (middle)/passive form and passive meaning, I am being captured; the future ἁλώσομαι is middle in form and has passive meaning, I will be captured; the aorist ἐάλων and perfect ἑάλωκα have active form and passive meaning (I was captured, I have been captured, respectively).

A FEW VERBS WITH ACTIVE PRESENTS USE THEIR AORIST PASSIVE FORMS IN A PECULIAR WAY:

φαίνω to show, has two aorist passive forms: first, ἐφάνθην and second, ἐφάνην. As is customary when of two alternative forms one is marked (first), and the other is not (second), the first form is a genuine aorist passive (I was shown), while the second, unmarked aorist passive  means I showed myself, I appeared.
