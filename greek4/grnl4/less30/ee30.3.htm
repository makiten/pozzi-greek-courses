30.3:  Dual number

 

In early stages of the Greek language, particularly in the Homeric epic, there were special endings for verbal and nominal forms referring to a pair.  These “dual” endings emphasized the close association of both elements, as in kinship, action in unison, or as a stylistic device that created a close pair where one did not exist.  Dual forms of verbs alternated with plural forms and disappeared from the language already in classical times.  Interestingly, in Hellenistic Greek a few dual forms were resurrected.

 

In conjugation

 

The dual does not have a first person, perhaps because it takes an external observer to perceive a pair as such.  Primary and secondary dual endings are always the same, so that the forms may theoretically be applied to any tense or mood.  Notice the similarity of active and middle/passive endings.

Examples

 

Dual subject, dual verb:

δύο ἄνδρες προσελθόντε Ἄγιφι διελεγέσθην μὴ ποιεῖν μάχην   Two men approached Agis and urged him not to bring on a battle.

 

Plural subject, dual verb:

δύο πυρὸς σώματα εἰς ἓν ξυνίστασθον εἶδος ἀέρος.  Two corpuscles of fire combine into one figure of air.
