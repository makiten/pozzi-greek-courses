31.3  Accusative absolute

 

The accusative absolute is a special version of the participle absolute construction that you learned as “genitive absolute.”  Only impersonal verbs usually have an accusative absolute, although the passive of a few verbs is found, as in the last example on this page. 

 

Impersonal verbs, by definition, cannot take a personal subject.  Their subject may be an infinitive or a noun clause, as in:

 

 δεῖ σε ποιεῖν ἃ ἂν κεκελευσμένα ᾖ. μὴ οὖν μέλλε.  You (sg) must do whatever has been commanded.

 

Instead of the 3rd person singular, in the accusative absolute we find the participle.  It stands to reason that the participle of an impersonal verb will be used only in the neuter singular.  The subject of this impersonal participle, again, may be an infinitive or a noun clause:

 

δέον σε τὰ κεκελευσμένα ποιεῖν, μὴ μέλλε. “It being necessary that you do what has been commanded, do not delay”  Since you must do what has been commanded, do not delay.

 

Similarly, ἐξόν ... permitted, χρεών, ... obligatory, δόξαν (aorist pple in the neuter sg) “it having seemed appropriate,” as it seemed appropriate, etc.

 

The accusative absolute, like the genitive absolute, is generally equivalent to an adverbial clause:

 

οὐδεὶς τὸ μεῖζον κακὸν αἱρήσεται ἑξὸν τὸ ἐλάττον (αἱρεῖσθαι) 

No one will choose the greater evil when it is possible to choose the lesser

 

εἰρημένον δ’ αὐταῖς ἀπαντᾶν ἐνθάδε... εὕυδουσι κοὐχ ἥκουσι.

Though it was told them to meet here, they sleep and have not come.
