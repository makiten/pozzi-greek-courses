Lesson 21.4: Clauses of fearing

 

These clauses, often grouped with other adverbial clauses (also in my charts, mea culpa),  are actually a special type of indirect statements.  With a main verb meaning “to fear” or something equivalent, the substance of the fear is expressed as follows.[1]

 

μή    +  Subjunctive  if there is fear or danger of something happening

 

μὴ οὐ   +  Subjunctive  if there is fear or danger of something not happening

 

Α formal translation would be:  “I fear / there is danger lest X  happens.”  In everyday style we say “I fear / there is danger that  X  happens / may, will, could, might, etc., happen.”

 

Examples:

                                                                                             clause of fearing

[ δέδοικα[2]]  (μή, ὥσπερ οἱ λωτοφάγοι, ἐπιλαθώμεθα[3] τῆς  οἴκαδε   ὁδοῦ.)

           V                                      ..............   comparison.............                    V in Subj                     gen.. .      adv        ... compl of V

 

I fear lest / that, like the Lotus-Eaters, we will / may / could forget the way home.

 

                                                                                             clause of fearing

[φοβεῖσθαι δεῖ ]  (μὴ οὐ τὴν ἐλευθερίαν διασῴζωμεν).

     infin = S           V

We must fear / be alert lest we do not preserve our freedom.

 

[1]  When the main verb is in the past, the clause of fearing may have  μὴ or  μὴ οὐ  + Optative instead.  However, we are not studying yet this substitution, which applies also to indirect discourse and to purpose clauses.  I did not mention this shift to the Optative before, because the mood had not been discussed.  The Subjunctive is, as usual in Greek, always correct in presenting a vivid utterance even in a past context.

[2]  A resultative perfect  = I am //very much// afraid.  The verb is  δείδω, δείσω, ἔδεισα, δέδοικα

[3]  ἐπι-λανθάνομαι  takes a gen complement.
