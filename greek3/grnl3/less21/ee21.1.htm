21.1  Aorist and Future of the Passive Voice 

You have been  warned  ( perhaps too often...) that the middle aorist and future cannot be used as passive.  In this lesson we will finally fill the gaps that our verb charts have been showing so far in those slots.  We start with the passive aorist, which is part of the citation of a verb: it is the sixth principal part. We use κωλύω  = to hinder, as a model

We construct the Aorist Passive of a regular verb as follows:

augment + present stem +  θη  + (surprise!)  active historical or secondary endings:

-ν, -σ, zero ending in the 3rd person,   μεν, τε, σαν.

Note: There is no thematic vowel in the Aorist Passive.

In the Indicative this formula results in:

 

 ἐ-κωλύ-θη-ν          

 ἐ-κωλύ-θη-ς

 ἐ-κωλύ-θη

 ἐ-κωλύ-θη-μεν

 ἐ-κωλύ-θη-τε

 ἐ-κωλύ-θη-σαν

To translate into English the Aorist Passive Indicative we put the verb to be in the past tense and add the past (passive) participle of the English verb:  I was hindered, etc.   In the Imperative and Subjunctive the Aorist has aspect but does not denote past time.  As to the Infinitive and Participle, in some contexts, as we have seen in the active voice, they may denote a time prior to the time of the main verb.

The Infinitive also shows this tense marker and adds the ending -ναι.   In the other moods, sometimes the tense marker shortens its vowel.  You will be asked this semester only to recognize these forms of the Aorist Passive, not to produce them.  These are the forms.  A few explanations follow to facilitate recognition.

AORIST PASSIVE PARTICIPLE

 

Let us create the aorist passive participle starting from the masculine.  As we did earlier when dealing with present and aorist active participles, and considering that the masculine belongs to the 3rd declension (as does the neuter), we begin by constructing its genitive. 

 

Since in the Indicative the Aorist Passive is disguised as an active tense, we use for the participle also the  consonantal group -ντ , which we found in active participles:   

The formula aorist passive  stem +  ντ  +  ending of the genitive singular  would produce the genitive  *κωλυ-θη-ντ-ος  

But, applying a phonetic change that we recognized earlier, namely, that  a long vowel is shortened before ντ, we must write κωλυ-θέ-ντ-ος 

In the masculine nominative singular this participle adds ντ , and with the well known loss of ντ   before  σ  and the ensuing compensatory lengthening, we obtain  κωλυ-θε-ντ-ς  > κωλυ-θείς

 FUTURE PASSIVE

 

We construct the Future Passive of a regular verb as follows: 

present stem +  θη  +   future mark σ  +  thematic vowel   +  (another surprise!)  middle primary endings:

 -μαι, -ῃ, -ται, -μεθα, -σθε, -νται.  

 

To translate into English the Future Passive Indicative we put the verb to be in the future tense and add the past (passive) participle of the English verb:  I will be hindered, etc.  The future has neither Imperative nor  Subjunctive..  As to the Infinitive and Participle, in some contexts, as we have seen in the active voice, they may denote a time later than the time of the main verb.
