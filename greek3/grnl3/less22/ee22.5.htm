22.5 Contrary-to-Fact Statements

 

1) Both in written and in oral communication we constantly make statements as representing facts (whether they do or not is beside the point; the grammar of a lie is the same as that of a true statement).  I am calling expressions such as “It is raining,” or “I am deeply saddened by your plight,” neutral or NON-COMMITTAL STATEMENTS.  The mood of the verb that presents them is, both in English and in Greek, the INDICATIVE.  

 

2) Some utterances  present their content as something possible, as a potentiality rather than as a fact.  These are STATEMENTS OF POSSIBILITY OR POTENTIAL STATEMENTS. In English we say “It may rain today,” “Perhaps I will come,” “They might / could / would[1] agree.”  Greek uses OPTATIVE + ἄν   to make potential statements.

 

3) Finally, we occasionally want to say that the content of a statement IS NOT or WAS NOT a fact.  These statements carry an implication: ...that the opposite IS or WAS the case.  They are “unreal” or “CONTRARY-TO-FACT“ because a condition that would make them feasible is not, or was not, fulfilled. We often include that unfulfilled condition in our language: “I would accept your offer //but I don’t//, if you were reliable //but you are not//.  “X would have won the race //but he didn’t// if he hadn’t broken a leg //but he did//.”

 

Greek does not have a special mood of the verb for this “unreality,” so to speak.  It resorts to PAST TENSES OF THE INDICATIVE MOOD ACCOMPANIED BY ἄν. 

 

The Imperfect + ἄν  expresses something that would be true now, but isn’t:

πάντες νῦν ἐπορευόμεθα ἄν οἴκαδε = We would all be going home now //but we don’t//.

 

The Aorist + ἄν  expresses something that would have been  true in the past, but wasn’t

ταῦτα ἐγένετο ἄν = That would have happened //but it did not//.
