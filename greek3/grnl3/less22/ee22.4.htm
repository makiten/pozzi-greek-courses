22.4 Overview of conditional clauses, 2

Potential conditions

You understand very well now that all conditional periods have a basic common logical structure: “If A, then B.”  They differ in the manner that the condition is presented: without suggesting whether it is feasible or not (Type I); as a future, i. e. possible, occurrence (Type II); as a customary occurrence (type V). 

The first chart given this semester, Overview 1 =  20.4, included only Types I, II, and V (partially), studied earlier. The current  Overview adds Type III ("should/would" conditions).  Overview 3 = 25.3 adds Contrary-to-fact conditions. 

In this lesson we add Type III, which we may  call a potential condition.  Yet because possibility is something that may occur in the future, Type III  is actually very similar to Type II.  Traditional grammars call Type II conditions (ἐάν + Subjunctive in the protasis, Future Indicative or equivalent in the apodosis) “Future more vivid conditions.”  More vivid, you say?  More vivid than what?  Type II conditions are more vivid than our new Type III, which is traditionally labelled “less vivid condition.”  So, if we think about conditions referring to the future, we have actually in the traditional terminology three degrees of “vividness.”

  • Most vivid conditions are simply our non-comittal conditions with Future Indicative in the protasis, Future indicative or equivalent in the apodosis.

  • More vivid conditions are our Type II conditions.

  • Less vivid conditions are our Type III.  In the apodosis they express possibility.  The construction that represents possibility in a main clause is  OPTATIVE + ἄν.  In the protasis  ἄν is not duplicated, so the complete formula is:

Optative

Optative +  ἄν

Examples of Type III conditions:

a)  εἰ βούλοιο τοῦτο ποιεῖν, ἄξιος ἂν εἴης τιμῆς.

If you (s) should want to do this, you would  deserve praise. 

 

b) (εἰ δὲ ἑαυτοὺς διακρίνοιμεν,) [οὐκ ἂν κρινοίμεθα ἀπ’ ἄλλων.]  

Should we  judge ourselves, we would not be judged by others.

 

c) ἁμαρτάνοις ἂν εἰ πάντα δοκοίης εἰδέναι.
 You would be wrong if you should think that you know everything.
