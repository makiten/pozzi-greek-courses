24.2α: Transitive and intransitive forms of ἵστημι

 

We begin by reviewing the distribution of active, middle, and passive forms of a regular verb, comparing the uses of English "stop" with Greek παύω.  We use the English verb "stop" both as transitive (bring someone or something to a halt) and intransitive (come to a halt, stand still, rest, etc.).  The passive uses are conversions of the transitive ones: I was stopped, they will be stopped by the police. 

In contrast, a Greek verb like παύω is less ambiguous because it has a vaster repertory of forms.

Let us now display the forms of ἵ-στη-μι, στή-σ-ω, ἔ-στη-σα / ἔ-στη-ν, ἕ-στη-κα, ἕ-στα-μαι, ἐ-στά-θη-ν.

I use the same colors to represent the different meanings, and I choose (for practical reasons, to avoid the ambiguity of English “to stand,” both transitive  and intransitive) the following translations:

 ACTIVE transitive MEANING = to erect; ACTIVE intransitive MEANING = to stand; MIDDLE TRANSITIVE MEANING = to erect for one’s own benefit; MIDDLE intransitive MEANING  = to make onself to stand, to stand up; PASSIVE MEANING= to be erected
