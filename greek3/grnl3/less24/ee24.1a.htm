24.1a:  Athematic verbs: a primer

 

Take a look at 3.3  In order to conjugate the Present Indicative of verbs with a first person ending in ω, you learned that its forms consist of three elements:  stem + thematic vowel + personal endings, and that in some of its persons the phonetic changes have sealed together, so to speak, the thematic vowel and the original ending.  You learned then the sequence of those thematic vowels: ο, ε, ε, ο, ε, ο.1   What has not been said yet, at least not overtly, is that ω verbs are only one of the two major groups of Greek verbs.  There is another "conjugation" (using the term in the sense that we say that there are three "declensions") in which the endings are applied to the stem without the intermediary of a thematic vowel.  They are called athematic verbs or –μι verbs

 

PLEASE DO NOT PANIC!  In this course it is not suggested that you memorize entire conjugations... If you commit to memory the Active Present Indicative of the two verbs on this page, and you understand the patterns, you will be able to form the Middle/Passive Present.  Focus on the "unpredictable" singular forms of the Active Imperfect, and you will have learned it; you will be able to construct also the corresponding Middle/Passive.  When you come across athematic forms in ancient Greek texts, you will recognize them, or at least, will be able to make educated guesses.  Furthermore, there remain only a few verbs belonging to this conjugation; the natural evolution of the language progressively "regularized" them or replaced them with ω counterparts.  Thus they are even fewer athematic verbs in koinê Greek.  It is also the case that these verbs were athematic only in the present (active and middle) systems, and (some of them) in the aorist (Active and Middle) systems, and even here they could not avoid  some thematic forms (for instance, the Subjunctive, for what is a Subjunctive without a long thematic vowel..?).

  

Below you will see the Active and Middle Present Indicative of two athematic verbs: 

τίθημι, θήσω, [ἔθηκα], τέθηκα, τέθεμαι, ἐτέθην          stem: θε / θη  = to place

δίδωμι, δώσω, [ἔδωκα], δέδωκα, δέδομαι, ἐδόθην         stem: δο / δω  = to give

They have, clearly, two different vowel stems.  Why is each vowel listed as short/long?  Because the short/long alternance is typical of these verbs.  In the singular active present indicative they have a long vowel, but a short vowel in the other numbers and persons.  The first person singular, i.e. the first principal part of the verb, ends  in -μι. 

 

The features of the three verbs addressed in this lesson may be summarized as follows.  For the special features of  ἵστημι, see 24.1b   

 

·                   The Present system is marked by reduplication.  While the reduplication of the perfect consists of the first consonant of the stem + ε, reduplication here is formed with the first consonant +  ι  :    τι-, δι-

 

·                   they have a special set of personal endings, some of which, however, are the same as those of ω verbs.

 

·               they alternate long and short  forms of the stem vowel, with a long in the Active Indicative Present and Imperfect singular, contrasted with a short form of the vowel in the Active plural and inall the forms of the middle / passive.  The 2nd and 3rd persons of the Imperfect singular of τίθημι , and the three persons of the Imperfect singular of δίδωμι, are irregular.  These endings may be explained in several ways.  The easiest is to say that a thematic vowel seems to have “intruded” in these and other forms that we will study later, and the long sounds ει  and ου (instead of  η and ω, respectively) result from ensuing contraction. 

 

·                   in the Subjunctive the stem vowel becomes short before another vowel and then contracts with it;

 

·                   in the Optative the stem vowel is short and forms a diphthong with the ι that marks this mood.

 

·               in the singular of the IMPERFECT, a thematic vowel “intrudes” and results in contractions.[1]
