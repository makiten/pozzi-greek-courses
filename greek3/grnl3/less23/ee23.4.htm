23.4  Translation of verbs: generalities, part 2 

How to translate deponent or semideponent verbs?

Deponent verbs, as you know, express an active meaning with (frequently) middle forms and occasionally with passive forms.  They pose a challenge to the translator.  Once again, we need to be attentive to context. 

 

a) Easiest to translate are deponents that use middle forms for active meanings in all their stems.  Their active voices are never found.  An example is

δέχομαι, δέξομαι, ἐδεξάμην, δέδεγμαι  to welcome, receive

 

 

 δέχομαι, δέξομαι, ἐδεξάμην, δέδεγμαι  to welcome, receive

present

  δέχῃ/δέχει με ὡς φίλον

   you (sg) welcome me as a friend

imperfect

   ἐδέχου με ὡς φίλον

   you (sg) used to welcome me as a friend

future

   δέξῃ με ὡς φίλον

   you (sg) will  welcome me as a friend

aorist

   ἐδέξω με ὡς φίλον

   you (sg) welcomed me as a friend

perfect

   δέδεχαι με ὡς φίλον

   you (sg) have  welcomed me as a friend

pluperfect

   ἐδέδεχο με ὡς φίλον

   you (sg) had  welcomed me as a friend

Some deponents choose the passive form for their aorist, e.g. ἀπεκρίθην = I answered, or ἐπορεύθην = I set out on a journey.

 

b) How do we translate an active  verb that has one deponent stem, (commonly) the future?  Following the citation, we translate all the forms as active.  Only the tenses with an active form will have a passive.  The passive of deponent tenses (as all deponent verbs) will have to be expressed with a synonym.

 

 

  ἀκούω, ἀκούσομαι, ἤκουσα, ἀκήκοα to hear

     Examples

present

   ἀκούουσιν τὰ ῥήματα

      they hear the words

imperfect

   ἤκουον τὰ ῥήματα

     they were hearing /  used to hear the words

future

   ἀκούσονται τὰ ῥήματα

     they will hear the words

aorist

   ἤκουσαν τὰ ῥήματα

    they heard the words  

perfect

   ἀκηκόασι τὰ ῥήματα

    they have heard the words

pluperfect

   ἠκηκόεσαν τὰ ῥήματα

     they had heard the words

 

c) How do we translate the middle voice (of a verb that has active forms as well).  How do we handle the passive of such verbs?  Apply one golden rule:  the passive forms mean passive voice of the active meaning, not of the middle!

πείθω, πείσω, ἔπεισα, πέπεικα, πέπεισμαι, ἐπείσθην  = to convince, persuade

with middle meanings: πείθομαι, πείσομαι, ἐπιθόμην, πέποιθα  = to obey (+ Dative)

The passive will mean "to be persuaded," not "to be obeyed."

 

Compare active and passive, in the different tenses:

 

 

 ACTIVE   MEANING

 PASSIVE  MEANING

 οἱ ῥήτορες ἡμᾶς πείθουσιν

 οἱ ῥήτορες ἡμᾶς  ἔπεισαν

  ... convinced us

ἐπείσθημεν  ὑπὸ τῶν ῥητόρων

 οἱ ῥήτορες ἡμᾶς πεπείκασιν
