23.3  Translation of verbs: generalities, part 1

I emphasize "generalities" because translation is not the application of a one-to-one correspondence.  We must always consider the context.   I illustrate the lists with simple examples.

 

I start with verbs used in independent sentences or main clauses. 

The main hurdles we need to negotiate are voice, mood, and tense.  The correspondences are intuitive.for a straightforward verb such as

κομίζω, κομίσω / κομιῶ, ἐκόμισα, κεκόμικα, κεκόμισμαι, ἐκομίσθην

If "he was carrying off all the prizes" sounds unidiomatic, we may translate "used to carry off ..." or "he carried off ..."

future

pluperfect

I had obtained money (as a reward)

The use of the middle voice faded considerably in Hellenistic and koinê Greek.  The general idea is that it expresses an action in which the subject is strongly involved, but this may happen in several respects:  γυμνάζομαι  = "I exercise" rather than "I train (others)," ἀλλάσσομαι = "I exchange something (acc) for something else (gen)," rather than  "I change," etc.  When it occurs, both in ancient and in later Greek, it is not always easy to perceive how the subject is involved.   Furthermore, in a language there is constant shifting and overlapping of meanings... so, more often than not you will need to look up the meaning in a lexicon.
