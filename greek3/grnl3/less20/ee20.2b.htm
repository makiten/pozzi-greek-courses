20.2b How to learn the Middle-Passive Perfect

 

If instead of applying the middle-passive perfect endings to a stem ending in a vowel or diphthong you must apply them to a stem ending in a consonant... you expect phonetic changes, and you find them in abundance.  Attempting to learn them by rote, especially at this stage of your study of the Greek language, would be self-defeating.

For the time being you can use confidently the following rules of thumb.  Start from the awareness that consonants in contact assimilate, i.e. become similar to each other. The sounds with which final consonants must assimilate, in the M-P Perfect Indicative, Pluperfect, and in the Perfect Participle, are: μ, σ, τ.

We may set aside right away the 3rd person pl of both tenses, where the endings are, respectively,  -νται and -ντο. This was an impossible challenge to the ears of Greek speakers, and they gave up. The 3rd person plural of the Middle-Passive Perfect and Pluperfect are formed by combining the corresponding participle with the verb to be, as we will see below.

Until you acquire more experience of the Greek language through your readings, expect to recognize rather than produce these forms.
