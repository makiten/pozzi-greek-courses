20.4 Overview of conditional (and concessive) clauses, 1

 

In this lesson we take a step back in order to look at conditional clauses, both to review the types we have studied so far and present those that we will study in detail in the next few lessons.  If you wish to refresh your memory about conditional clauses as a special (and important!) group of adverbial clauses,  go to 13.3, 14.4. In 16.4 you can see conditions listed as one type among adverbial clauses that answer specific questions such as  when? (temporal clauses),  why? (temporal clauses), etc.  The question that elicits a condition is seldom asked, but it would be "upon which condition?"  These are all circumstances of the action of the main verb. 

Let us take a closer look now at conditional clauses themselves. How are they marked?  In English they are "if...then" clauses.  In Greek they are introducted  by " εἰ = if," or " εἰ μή = unless."  (It is useful to remember that negatives in conditional clauses are always  μή or its compounds, never  οὐ).  If you look at their internal structure, though, you find different patterns: indicative,  ἄν + Subjunctive, a mood that we have not studied yet, the Optative, etc. These varieties of their internal syntax show how conditions are presented by the speaker or writer, who may for example be aware that the condition he is stating is, or has been, unfulfilled.  We will simplify the classification of conditions as follows.

Concessive clauses

Same syntax as conditional clauses, with καί  = “even” in the dependent clause (κἄν  when combined with ἄν). In English, concessive clauses are introduced by “though,” “although,” “even if.”

 

 

EXAMPLES

I) Simple (non-committal) condition 

εὐτυχεῖς ἐσμεν εἰ ἡ στρατιὰ ἐν τῇ πόλει ἐστίν.     We are fortunate if the army is in the city.

εἰ ταῦτα λέξεις, ἐχθαρῶ σε.     If you (will) say this, I shall hate you.

Note that the speaker does not hint whether the condition is real, feasible, contrary-to-fact, etc.  All that is stated is the connection between the condition as a cause and its consequence. 

 

II) Future condition ("more vivid")

ἐὰν μὴ δέξησθε τὴν μαρτυρίαν ἡμῶν, οὐ σωθήσεσθε.

If you (pl) will not accept our witness, you will not be saved.  or  Unless you accept our witness, you ...

Note: It would be self-defeating to define the way that these future conditions differ from simple  future conditions with the verb in the Future Indicative.  When it is possible to perceive such a difference, it will be given by the context.  Future Indicative conditions, traditionally dubbed "most  vivid conditions," do not deserve to be considered a separate type: they belong to Type I above.

 

V) Generalizing condition

τὸν ἀδικοῦντα, ἐὰν λάβωμεν, κολάζομεν.

If we capture a wrongdoer, we (always) punish him.

Note: Generalizing conditions are typical, e.g., of decrees, which are meant to be always valid.

 

EXAMPLES OF CONCESSIVE CLAUSES:

Simple (Type I) φήσουσι δή με σοφὸν εἶναι, εἰ καὶ μή εἰμι.

They will indeed say that I am wise, although I am not.

Generalizing (Type V)  οἱ θεοὶ ἱκανοί εἰσι τοὺς μικρούς, κἂν ἐν δεινοῖς ὦσι, σῴζειν.

The gods are //always// capable of saving small men, even if they are in dire circumstances.

 

Footnotes

1) We have inherited these and other definitions from centuries-old grammars.  Even though we are simplifying the classification, it is necessary to mention these terms so you may be able to handle in the future the traditional reference materials.

2) Compare the generalizing temporal ("whenever") and relative ("whoever, whatever") clauses, also with ἄν + Subjunctive.
