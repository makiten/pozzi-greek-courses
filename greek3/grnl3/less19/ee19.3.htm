19.3  Purpose clauses

Without having exhausted the varieties of temporal and conditional clauses (because that would encumber excessively this introductory course), we introduce now an important type of dependent clauses: clauses of purpose (sometimes called final clauses). To review those types studied previously (temporal, relative, and conditional),  go to 14.4, 16.4.

 

Purpose clauses

In English we introduce the purpose of a verbal action with: "in order to...," "so as to...," or with the simple infinitive "to...,'  as in "I left early to get tickets."

This use of the infinitive became frequent in koinê Greek.

Also in koinê Greek, purpose is often expressed with the genitive of the infinitive:  τοῦ φυγεῖν = in order to flee , τοῦ πλεῦσαι = in order to sail.  This construction is found in ancient Greek, but it tends to be accompanied by ἕνεκα = for the sake of, i. e. in order to...

 

The typical structure of a clause of purpose calls for a subordinating conjunction introducing a verb in the Subjunctive:

 ἵνα / ὅπως / ὡς     +    SUBJUNCTIVE    Negation:   μή                                

EXAMPLES                                                                                                               

a) [  ὁ ἱερεὺς εὔχεται ] ( ἵνα πάντες σῴζωνται.)     

  The priest prays so that all may be saved. 

                                                  purpose clause                                                          purpose clause

b) [  ἐσθίωμεν ]  (   ὅπως ζώσωμεν·)   [ μὴ ζῶμεν ] (  ὅπως ἐσθίωμεν )

Let us eat in order to live.  Let us not live in order to eat.

Note:   ἐσθίωμεν and  ζῶμεν  are independent subjunctives (volitional, see 17.3).
