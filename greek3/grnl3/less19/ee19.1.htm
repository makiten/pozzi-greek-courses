19.1 Perfective aspect

To the two aspects considered so far, progressive and punctual, we add now a third: perfective aspect. How are the three aspects represented by the different verbal stems? I illustrate this with the verb πορεύω = “το carry, ferry someone or something over, e.g. a river.”

 

 

Progressive

Punctual

Perfective

PRESENT

 πορεύω

The present may be progressive or punctual:

I ferry, I am ferrying

 

IMPERFECT

ἐπόρευον

Progressive:

I was ferrying1

 

 

FUTURE

πορεύσω

The future may be progressive or punctual:

I will ferry, I will be ferrying

 

AORIST

ἐπόρευσα

 

Punctual: I ferried

 

PERFECT

πεπόρευκα

 

 

I have ferried

A “perfect” tense represents a “perfected,” i.e. completed, action. The past action itself, however, is not the focus of this tense: its present result is. Some Greek perfects are best translated with an English present, e.g.  ἕστηκα = I stand;  τέθνηκε = he is dead.   A rule of thumb for the translation of Greek perfect tenses into English is this: they correspond to English perfect tenses, formed with the auxiliary “have” and the past participle of the verb conjugated. So active  πεφύλακα = “I have watched” (someone or something); middle or passive  πεφύλαγμαι = “I have defended myself” (as middle), or “I have been watched” (as passive). The perfect may be transposed to the past, it has its own imperfect, as it were, known as “Pluperfect”: active  ἐπεφυλάκη = I had watched (someone or something); middle or passive  ἐπεφυλάγμην = “I had defended myself” (as middle), or “I had been watched” (as passive).

Notes:

1) The imperfect may also denote a customary action = I used to ferry.

2) The accusative may be used adverbially, without a preposition. A common application is exemplified by τέλος , the neuter sg accusative of a noun, more often of an adjective, used as an adverb. That is the origin of many of the adverbs that we have encountered so far:  μόνον, πρῶτον, etc.

In turn, τρεῖς ἡμέρας  is an example of a more specific adverbial use of the accusative, to denote duration; a parallel use for space denotes extension.
