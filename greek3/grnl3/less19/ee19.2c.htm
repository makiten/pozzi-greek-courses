19.2.c How to learn the Active Perfect

 

Even a cursory glance at the citation forms of the verbs in this lesson will show that not all perfect stems are predictable. In this introductory course it will be sufficient to acquire a few strategies to be able to recognize and translate its forms.

So far we have been handling only verbs with a present stem ending in a diphthong or a vowel, e.g.  παύ-ω, τιμά-ω, etc. In these cases one can apply the tense suffix -κα  to a present stem ending in a diphthong, such as  παυ-, without any changes:  πέπαυκα, or to the stem of a contracted verb in  -άω, έω, and  -όω (that we will study later), by lengthening the vowel that contracts in the present system and is lengthened also in the future and in the first aorist. E.g., for  τιμάω, τιμήσω, ἐτίμησα, we obtain  τετίμηκα.

But not all stems end in vowels or diphthongs.

What if the stem ends in a consonant? Since there is no thematic vowel in the perfect, that consonant will, obviously, encounter κ. What we need to do is apply straightforward phonetic changes. For a basic review of consonant changes, go to Rev0.4.2

It is useful to recognize that:

  • If the present stem ends in a dental stop, it drops before  -κ just as it does before  -σ of the future or aorist:

 θαυμάζω, θαυμάσω, ἐθαύμασα, τεθαύμακα

The nasal dental  -ν  becomes  γ (pronounced as nasal) before -κ:    

present  φαίνω   perfect    πέφαγκα

All that having been said, here is a rule of thumb:

 

In the fourth principal part of the verb, identify the stem by cutting away  -α, and then you will recognize that the perfect, whether first or second, and no matter how unpredictable, follows the regular model.
