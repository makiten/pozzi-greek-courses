27.1: Review of the use of the moods in independent sentences and main clauses

 

The chart in 22.6 is expanded here partially.  Other specifics will be added in the lessons of the fourth semester.:

 

Added in this version:

 

On the indicative

 

Impersonal verbs such as δεῖ  or χρή  use imperfect without  ἄν for a contrary-to-fact expression of

obligation or necessity:

 

X ought to be done (but isn’t), with a present infinitive:  

ἔδει τὸν ἄρχοντα βουλεύεσθαι πρὶν πολέμου ἄρξαι

The ruler ought to hold consultations before starting a war.

 X ought to be have been done (but wasn’t), with an aorist infinitive: : 

 ἔδει τοὺς ἄρχοντας  βουλεύσασθαι  πρὶν πολέμου ἄρξαι

            The rulers ought to have held consultations before starting a war

 

On the subjunctive

 

 

Probably because they hide implicit clauses of fearing, the combinations οὐ μή  and μή οὐ  +  the subjunctive express, respectively, categorical statements and tentative statements::

 

οὐ μή τύχωσι πάντων ἃ ἐπιθυμοῦσι 

 They certainly will not achieve everything they desire.

μή οὐ  ῥάδιον ᾖ πάντων ἃ ἐπιθυμεῖς τυχεῖν.  

I fear that it is not easy to achieve everything you (sg) desire. (Perhaps it is not easy...)

                                                              

INDEPENDENT USES OF THE FOUR MOODS 

 

1.      STATEMENTS OR QUESTIONS

 

Indicative

negation οὐ

Subjunctive

negation μή

Optative

 

NON-COMMITTAL STATEMENTS

νόμους τίθενται  They establish laws.

CATEGORICAL STATEMENTS

οὐ * μή λύωσι τὴν δημοκρατίαν

 

They  certainly will not destroy democracy

 

TENTATIVE STATEMENTS:

* μή οὐ λύωσι τὴν δημοκρατίαν

 

Perhaps they may destroy democracy.

*  Supply  // φοβοῦμαι//

OPTATIVE  + ἂν  : POTENTIAL

negation  οὐ   

 

τίς ἂν βούλοιτο δοῦλος

γενέσθαι;

 

  Who would want to become a slave?

CONTRAFACTUAL STATEMENTS

 ἂν + PAST TENSES OF THE INDICATIVE

νόμους ἂν ἐτίθεντο

They would establish laws (but do not)

νόμους ἂν ἔθεντο

They would have established  laws (but did not)

Impersonal verbs may be contrafactual in the imperfect (without ἂν)

ἔδει ὑμᾶς νόμους τίθεσθαι

You (pl) ought to  establish laws

(with present infinitive)

ἔδει ὑμᾶς νόμους θέσθαι

You (pl) ought to  have established  laws

 (with aorist infinitive)

 

 

 

VOLITIONAL UTTERANCES: COMMANDS, PROHIBITIONS, PRAYERS, POLITE REQUESTS

 

 

Imperative

negation μή

VOLITIONAL SUBJUNCTIVE

negation μή

 

 

Optative

 

2nd and 3rd persons commands or

prohitibions:

 

VARIOUS TENSES OF THE IMPERATIVE MAY BE INDISTINGUISHABLE IN MEANING, OR MAY DIFFER IN ASPECT OR IN THE COGENCY OF THE COMMAND 

(PROHIBITION: μή + PRESENT IMPERATIVE)

 

Present: παῦε /  μηδὲν φοβοῦ

Stop!  Do not fear anything!

Aorist: παῦσαι / μηδὲν φόβησαι

Stop!  Do not fear anything!

Perfect : πέπαυσο  /

 μηδὲν πεφοβημένος ἴσθι

(more emphatic)

Stop immediately!  Have no fear!

 

 

AN ALTERNATIVE FORM OF PROHIBITION:

μή + AORIST SUBJUNCTIVE

 

μὴ φροντίσῃς

 

Do not worry!

 

 

HORTATORY  SUBJUNCTIVE:

 

Usually in the 1st person plural:

 

ἀκούσωμεν τοῦ ἀνδρός

 

Let us listen to the man!

 

OPTATIVE  + ἂν  : POTENTIAL

negation  οὐ   

 

POLITE REQUESTS:

 

δείξαις ἄν μοι τίς ἠ εἰς ἄστυ ὁδὸς;

 

Would you please show me which //is// the way to the city?

 

    

 

 

 

2.      EXPRESSION OF THE SPEAKER’S STATE OF MIND

 

 

VOLITIONAL SUBJUNCTIVE

negation μή

 

 

Optative

 

DELIBERATIVE  SUBJUNCTIVE:

1st person sg and pl: = shall I...?  shall we...?

 

 πάντα λέγωμεν, ἢ σιγῶμεν; τί ποιῶμεν;

 

Shall we tell everything, or keep silent?  What must we do?

 

 

OPTATIVE ALONE:  WISH

negation μή

 

εἴθε γιγνώσκοις αὐτόν

 

May you know him! (English makes a statement to express this type of feasible wish: “I wish you get to know him!”)

 

μὴ ψεύδοιτο ἀλλὰ λέγοι τἀληθῆ.

 

May he not lie, but tell the truth.
