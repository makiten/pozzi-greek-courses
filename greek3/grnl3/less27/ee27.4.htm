27.4: Causal clauses, Comparative clauses, and Clauses of "effort" or "striving"

 

CAUSAL CLAUSES

 

The negation is οὐ

 

Causal clauses are introduced by these subordinating conjunctions: ὅτι = because (it may also introduce an indirect statement),  and διότι = because; ἐπεί and ἐπειδή, both of which may introduce temporal clauses as well (compare English “since”), and the ubiquitous ὡς = as.

 

The verbs of causal clauses are usually in the Indicative, with causes being adduced as statements of fact.  They may be expressed, however, also as possibilities (potential optative with ἄν) or as unreal (past indicative with ἄν).

 

When the main verb is in a past tense, a causal clause may have the form of direct discourse or the indirect of oblique optative.  In other words, causal clauses may behave like indirect statements.

 

EXAMPLE

ἐπεὶ δὲ ὑμει̂ς οὐ βούλεσθε συμπορεύεσθαι, ἀνάγκη δή μοι ... ὑμα̂ς προδόντα τῃ̂ Κύρου φιλίᾳ χρη̂σθαι (from Xen.Anab.1.3.5)

... but since you do not wish to continue the march with me, I must retain the friendship of Cyrus by renouncing you,.
 

COMPARATIVE CLAUSES

 

Comparative clauses are introduced by ὡς, ὥσπερ, καθώς.  The negation is οὐ

 

α) They have a verb, which most of the time is in the Indicative or in the same mood and tense as the verb of the main clause; it may be represented by an infinitive of indirect discourse, as in these two examples: 

 

οὐκ ἔδει καὶ σὲ ἐλεῆσαι τὸν σύνδουλόν σου, ὡς κἀγὼ σὲ ἠλέησα;   (Matthew 18.33)

Shouldn't you also have had mercy on your fellow servant, even as I had mercy on you?

 

Neoptolemos says to Philoctetes in Sophocles, Philoctetes 1395-96

 

...ῥᾷστ’ ἐμοὶ μὲν τῶν λόγων λήξαι, σὲ δὲ ζῆν, ὥσπερ ἤδη ζῇς, ἄνευ σωτηρίας.
...the easiest course for me is to be silent and (let) you live without help, as before.

 

With a different verb: 

ἔστιν γὰρ  οὕτως ὥσπερ οὗτος ἐννέπει

For it is so even as he says (Sophocles, Trachiniae 475)

 

b) Some comparative clauses are introduced by what is actually a relative pronoun, correlative to a demonstrative usually included in the main clause.

 

Connoting size or quantity: 

 

τοσοῦτος, τοσαύτη, τοσοῦτο                    ὅσος, η, ον  

= so large, (pl) so many                                                      = as  (rather than “as large,” “as many”)

 

Connoting quality:                                              

 

τοιοῦτος, τοιαύτη, τοιοῦτο = such                οἷος, οἵα, οἷον  = as

 

 

EXAMPLES:

 

τὸ δῶρον ἐστὶ τοιοῦτο (οἷον ἤλπιζον).

The gift is such as I was hoping.

 

ἆρ’ ἐστί σοι νῦν τοσαῦτα χρήματα (ὅσα ἐβούλου πράξεσθαι);

Do you have now as much money as you wanted to earn?

 

c) To avoid repetition the verb may be implicit.  These are the comparative constructions (usually marked in the keys as “comparisons”) included in the texts of this course since the first semester.  The pattern is: 

 

ὡς, ὥσπερ, καθώς  +  the same case the first element of comparison had.

 

...ἐὰν σοὶ συνδοκῇ ὥσπερ ἐμοί.

... If  it seems good also to you as (it seems) to me    (P. Phaedo. 100c).

 

The  verb is also omitted in Iliad 6. 466-67, where ὥσπερ, as is the case often in Homer, is divided by an intervening word:

 

Ζεῦ ἄλλοι τε θεοὶ δότε δὴ καὶ τόνδε γενέσθαι

παῖδ’ ἐμὸν ὡς καὶ ἐγώ περ ἀριπρεπέα Τρώεσσιν...

Zeus and ye other gods, grant that this my child may likewise prove, even as I, pre-eminent amid the Trojans

 

c) Some comparative clauses have special meanings:

·        If ὡς means "as if" the verb of the comparative clause may not  be in the Indicative:  

ταῦτα προσδέχοιτ’ ἂν ὡς ἂν οἰκεῖα //εἴη// [Theophrastus.Characters 1.16.12]

·        They may take constructions similar to those of conditional clauses, e.g. generalizing

 

τὸ μὲν γὰρ πέρας, ὡς ἂν ὁ δαίμων βουληθῇ, πάντων γίγνεται [Demosthenes 18.92]

For the end of all events happens as the god wills

 

CLAUSES OF EFFORT OR STRIVING

 

These clauses depend on a few special verbs, meaning to see to it that..., to take care that..., to do one's best so that...  Conceptually, they differ from simple purpose clauses in that they present that purpose also as a direct object of the main verb.  It is a purpose, we understand, that will be achieved.   Examples in English:  See to it that no one disturbs me!  They took care that everyone would be happy.

 

χρημάτων οὖν οὐκ αἰσχύνῃ ἐπιμελούμενος ὅπως σοὶ ἔσται ὡς πλεῖστα, καὶ  δόξης καὶ τιμῆς, φρονήσεως δὲ καὶ ἀληθείας καὶ τῆς ψυχῆς ὅπως ὡς βελτίστη ἔσται οὐκ ἐπιμελῇ οὐδὲ φροντίζεις;    [Plato, Apology 29e]

Are you not ashamed to care for the acquisition of wealth //making sure that// you will have as much as possible, and for reputation and honor, when you neither care nor take thought for wisdom and truth and //make sure that// it is as perfect as possible?
